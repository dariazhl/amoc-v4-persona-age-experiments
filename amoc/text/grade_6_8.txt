Clays and shales suitable for use in the manufacture of brick are found in many locations all over the world. These materials are the result of chemical disintegration and the erosion of rocks due to weathering over millions of years since the earth's formation. On the surface of the earth the broken particles have been carried away in some cases by glacial movements but generally by rains and floods and deposited as sediment on the beds of streams, lakes and oceans. The products thus formed through the geological ages are classified in three groups as follows: surface clays, shales and fire clays. The first evidence of clay's being used for construction dates back to approximately 4000 B.c. This was a clay tablet evidently patted into shape by hand and dried in the sun. The making of brick gradually improved in quality; there are evidences of straw being added to the clay to obtain more adhesive qualities. As time passed, man discovered that by using fire he could obtain a more durable and a harder brick. With this new discovery, brick usage spread quickly from the Mesopotamian plains eastward to Persia, India and China and westward into Egypt, Rome, Greece and finally was introduced by the Romans into England. The English were quick to see its possibilities. About the time of Henry Viii, the use of brickwork began to attain a degree of excellence, and after its great fire, London was rebuilt of brick. The manufacture and use of brick were continually improved and, in the New World, brick imported from Europe was used in much of the construction.
